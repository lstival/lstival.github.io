My research focuses on advancing **self-supervised learning (SSL)** and **representation learning** for complex and **multimodal data**. I investigate how state-of-the-art SSL methods — originally developed for vision and language — can be extended to **time series**, **remote sensing**, and other **domain-specific data**. This involves developing training strategies that capture temporal dependencies, cross-modal relations, and contextual patterns without the need for labeled data, allowing models to autonomously learn meaningful structure from diverse sources.

A central component of my work is the exploration of **SSL frameworks** that integrate modern architectures such as **attention mechanisms**, **transformers**, and **state-space models (SSMs)**. These approaches provide flexible and efficient representations capable of modeling long-term dependencies and dynamic behaviors. By combining ideas from **differential equations**, **signal processing**, and **neural attention**, I aim to design systems that are both theoretically grounded and computationally scalable for large-scale temporal and multimodal datasets.

Beyond algorithmic development, my research focuses on **applying SSL and machine learning methods across different domains** to enhance performance and generate new insights from experimental data. This includes adapting multimodal SSL models to extract cross-domain representations, improving model generalization, and revealing underlying patterns that traditional techniques fail to capture. Ultimately, my goal is to create **generalizable and interpretable learning frameworks** that bridge modern machine learning with domain-specific discovery and analysis.
